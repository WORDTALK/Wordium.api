Languages, Dialects, and Labels

Over here at sunny (and dry) Wordset headquarters, we've been quietyly chugging away working on our Wordset 2.0 milestones. These changes aren't about big visual updates to the site or new buttons to hit everywhere, what we're doing is setting the stage for Wordset to equitably and fairly start cateloguing language. That's why we're adding support for multiple-languages, dialect tagging, and usage labels.

Most of the code now supports multiple languages. We recently changed the URLs of the words from "/word/sushi" to "/en/sushi", so that we can provide the same website, and eventually handle all the language codes we want. There are still some technical edge cases to work out on the site, but the real thing we need to launch in our first language is volunteers! We, unfortunately, only speak English natively, so we need native and fluent speakers of other languages to help do the hard work of getting a first version going. If you know someone who would be willing to help, we have a quick form that we'd love for you to fill out!

Dialects are important to us. Actually, one of the founders speaks British English, and the other American English, and so we can't just very well go around making a dictionary that only supports one of those! But, we also know that language is far more varied than just the two major branches of English. There are dialects like Canadian English and AAVE, that are often overlooked in many dictionaries and we don't want to be like that.
