Languages, Dialects, and Labels

Over here at sunny (and dry) Wordset headquarters, we've been quietly chugging away working on our Wordset 2.0 milestones. These changes aren't about big visual updates to the site or new buttons to hit everywhere. What we're doing is setting the stage for Wordset to start equitably and fairly cataloguing language. That's why we're adding support for multiple languages, dialect tagging, and usage labels.

Most of the code now supports multiple languages. You may have noticed that we recently changed the URLs of words from "/word/sushi" to "/en/sushi", so that we can use one website to handle all the languages we want to support. There are still some technical edge cases to work out, but the real thing we need to launch our second language is volunteers! Unfortunately, we only speak English natively. So we need native and fluent speakers of other languages to help do the hard work of getting a first version of other languages going. If you or someone you know would be willing to help, we have a quick form about contributing that we'd love for you to fill out!

Dialects are important to us. One of the founders speaks British English, the other American English, and so we can't just go around making a dictionary that only supports one of those! It's not a perfect dialect representation, but we think it will work well enough for now. We're also introducing other labels for words that can help with usage. Here's our initial set of non-dialect labels:

Technical
Terms or meanings of terms particular to specific professional disciplines.
Scientific
Words specific to scientific disciplines, for example physics or chemistry.
Medical
Terms that refer to various conditions or parts of the human body, devices used in medical treatment and so on.
Legal
Words that have a specific meaning in a legal context.
Formal
Words that are typically used in a more serious setting, often to indicate a certain expertise, for example in job application or an academic essay.
Idiom
An expression whose meaning cannot be inferred from the meanings of the words that make it up. In other words, phrases that would make an English learner go "huh?" The British idiom "for donkey's years" meaning "for a long time" is a good example.
Archaic
Words that aren't used anymore and/or words that have an older meaning that is no longer in popular use.
Informal
Casual language used in everyday life, especially among friends and acquaintances.
Slang
All slang is informal. Particular to slag is its tendency to give common words alternate meanings, like dough for money and ace for awesome.
Offensive
Language that is likely to make its recipient feel disrespected, marginalized, or otherwise upset.

Labels are super easy to propose on meanings right now... just click the edit meaning button and select what labels you would like to apply.

This is the first part of our dialect addition. We're now working on having multiple spellings of words too. This will help you find words you aren't sure how to spell, as well as help us compile a list of the most common misspellings.

Now that you've got all the latest intel, feel free to go out there and start labeling! You can taken part in the current parentheses roundup, pick a random word, or go after whatever word you want with the search bar above. Happy wordsetting :)
